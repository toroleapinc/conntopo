Does the wiring of the human brain actually matter for neural computation — or could random wiring work just as well?

We tested this systematically, and the answer surprised even us.

Using real structural connectivity data from the Human Connectome Project (76 brain regions, 1,560 connections), we compared neural dynamics on the actual brain wiring against 5 types of random networks using two independent dynamics models.

Key findings:

1. The real connectome produces MASSIVELY different dynamics — effect sizes of Cohen's d = 5-2400+
2. In the Wilson-Cowan model, random wiring literally collapses to silence while the real connectome sustains oscillations
3. Even rewiring that preserves the same number of connections per region produces different dynamics — it's the SPECIFIC pattern that matters
4. The effect is robust across both Kuramoto and Wilson-Cowan models

This extends the landmark Drosophila connectome work (Nature 2024) to the human macro-scale: the brain's topology isn't just structural scaffolding — it actively shapes computation.
